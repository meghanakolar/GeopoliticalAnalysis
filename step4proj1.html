Exploring our Data

The dataset used in this study was put together to conduct research on political transitions for countries around the world. It’s purpose is to collect information on regime characteristics, specifically focusing on how a country’s political system changes throughout the years. The dataset consists of 14,768 observations and 43 variables. Each row represents political information for one country on a given year, ranging from 1950 to 2020. The variables provide information on whether a country is democratic, its regime type, and information on elections, leaders, suffrage, power changes, and colonial history.

Link to original dataset: https://www.christianbjoernskov.com/bjoernskovrodedata/

From 1980 to 2020, gender differences in the age at which leaders take office vary between presidents and monarchs. Among presidents, the gap is small and relatively stable, with men only slightly older than women at the time they take office. Both groups also show a gradual increase in age over time, suggesting that gender plays little role in when presidents begin their terms. In contrast, monarchs show a much larger and growing gap. Male monarchs tend to take the throne at older ages, while female monarchs are generally younger, with their ages even decreasing in more recent years. Overall, gender disparities in age at taking office are minimal in elected systems but much more pronounced in monarchical ones, where succession patterns create stronger divides.

Adding More Information

To explore our research questions further, we added more variables to our original dataset. We wanted to identify the nominal GDP (measured in US dollars) and the proportion of women in parliament for each country and year. From this https://api-ninjas.com/api/gdp API, we extracted the nominal GDP of each country and added it into our dataset. We also gathered the proportion of women in parliament from this source https://databank.worldbank.org/source/sustainable-development-goals-(sdgs)#.

Democracies tend to have slightly higher representation of women in national parliaments compared to non-democracies. The median percentage is higher for democracies (15.8%) than for non-democracies (14%), and the mean is also somewhat higher (17.7% vs. 16%).

However, the distributions are fairly similar overall, with substantial overlap between the two groups’ boxplots. Both democracies and non-democracies show wide variability and several outliers. Non-democratic countries seem to have more outliers, including some at higher values, with two outliers exceeding 60%.
